# Playbook: job-description

**Trigger:** "run the job-description playbook for [role title]"

**When to use:** Before posting any role. Run this instead of writing a JD from scratch or copying a template from the internet.

---

## What it does

1. Read your company stage, team size, culture notes, active ventures, and north star metric from `CLAUDE.md`.
2. Read any existing hiring notes or previous JDs in the vault.
3. Ask (if not already clear): What problem will this person solve in the first 90 days? What does success look like at 6 months?
4. Write a job description structured as:

   **The role in one sentence:** What this person will own, not what they'll do day to day.

   **Why this role matters right now:** The specific company context — what's happening that makes this hire urgent. Not generic. Specific to the current stage and goals.

   **What you'll do in the first 90 days:** 3-5 concrete outcomes, not responsibilities. "Set up our outbound motion and generate the first 10 qualified pipeline deals" not "responsible for outbound sales."

   **What we're looking for:** 4-5 things that actually matter for this role at this stage. Not a laundry list. If every item is a must-have, nothing is.

   **What we offer:** Compensation range (be specific — vague ranges waste everyone's time), equity range and how it's structured, stage of company, what makes this an interesting problem to work on.

   **What we don't offer:** Be honest about what the role isn't. A chaotic early-stage environment, no established playbook, high ambiguity. The right person wants to know this. The wrong person is filtered out.

5. Save to `Process/hiring/[role]-JD-[date].md` and link to any related hiring notes.

**Output format:** `Process/hiring/[role]-JD-YYYY-MM-DD.md`

**Notes:**
- Write for the person you actually want, not a generic ideal. The best candidates can tell the difference in 30 seconds.
- If the role is "figure it out yourself" — say that. Some people want exactly that.
- Never list more than 5 requirements. Every additional requirement reduces applicant quality, not quantity.
